\section{Critical Paper Review}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Q1: Primary Contributions}

The paper addresses an under-explored problem in NLP: detecting Patronising and Condescending Language (PCL) towards vulnerable communities in media discourse \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
The authors introduced the \textit{Don't Patronize Me!} dataset, containing 10637 paragraphs concerning vulnerable social groups, annotated for both PCL presence and specific PCL categories \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
The authors evaluate several baseline models, including SVM-based methods, BiLSTM, and BERT variants, with RoBERTa-base achieving the best performance on binary PCL classification \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
The authors also proposed a taxonomy of PCL categories, aimed to help better understand the different forms of PCL and to guide future research \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.

\subsection{Q2: Technical Strengths}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item The dataset draws from news sources across 20 English-speaking countries and covers 10 distinct vulnerability keywords, ensuring diversity and representativeness \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
    \item The two step annotation scheme, which first identifies PCL presence and then categorises PCL spans, enables both binary classification and fine-grained category analysis \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
    \item The annotation process used a rigorous two-step methodology, where the first step involved identifying the presence of PCL and the second step involved categorising the type of PCL, which allows for more detailed analysis.
    \item The paper establishes clear benchmarks through comprehensive evaluation of both traditional methods (SVM, BiLSTM) and transformer-based models (BERT, RoBERTa, DistilBERT) \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Q3: Key Weaknesses}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item The dataset is relatively small for training large transformer-based models, particularly for underrepresented categories (e.g., \textit{"The poorer, the merrier"} contains only 64 samples), risking overfitting and limiting generalisation \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}.
    \item The experiments use only paragraph-level classification, the available span-level annotations are not utilised for training, potentially limiting the models' ability to learn fine-grained PCL patterns.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Analysis}

The basic statistical profiling reveals concrete structural challenges in the PCL dataset:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Severe Class Imbalance.} Exactly 7{,}581 of the 8{,}375 training samples (90.5\%) are Non-PCL, leaving only 794 samples (9.5\%) as positive PCL examples, yielding a $\sim$9.5:1 imbalance. A trivial classifier predicting ``Non-PCL'' for every input would achieve 90.5\% accuracy while having zero utility.

    \item \textbf{Within-PCL Label Sparsity.} Among the 7 PCL categories (Labels 0--6), Label\_0 is the most frequent (574 samples, 6.85\%) and Label\_5 the second most (363, 4.33\%), while Label\_6 is extremely rare with only 29 instances (0.35\%). For the binary task all labelled samples are treated uniformly as PCL\,=\,1, but this internal skew is relevant if fine-grained multi-label classification is attempted later.

    \item \textbf{Label Co-occurrence.} Of the 794 PCL samples, 551 (69.4\%) carry more than one label, with individual samples carrying up to 5 labels simultaneously. When a text is patronising, it typically exhibits multiple PCL patterns at once, indicating PCL is a multi-faceted linguistic phenomenon rather than a single discrete signal.

    \item \textbf{Right-Skewed Token Distribution.} The distribution has a median of 38 tokens, a mean of 44.18 (std 26.85), and a maximum of 820 tokens. Most samples are short-to-medium paragraphs, but a long tail of substantially longer passages must be accommodated.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Impact Statement}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Metric Selection.} With 90.5\% Non-PCL, accuracy is actively misleading. Optimisation must target the \textbf{positive-class (PCL) F1} directly. A threshold sweep on the dev set is also necessary, as the default 0.5 decision boundary is biased toward the majority class.

    \item \textbf{Loss Function.} Standard Binary Cross-Entropy will be overwhelmingly biased toward predicting ``Non-PCL''. \textbf{Weighted Binary Cross-Entropy} with {\texttt{pos\_weight}}\,$\approx$\,9.5 (reflecting the 9.5:1 ratio) or \textbf{Focal Loss} must be used to redirect gradient updates toward the rare PCL cases.

    \item \textbf{Sampling Strategy.} With a 1:9.5 ratio, a random mini-batch of 32 samples contains on average only $\sim$3 PCL examples, resulting in an extremely noisy training signal. \textbf{Weighted batch sampling} to oversample PCL positives is required to guarantee a consistent minority-class signal across training steps.

    \item \textbf{Sequence Length.} The median of 38 tokens sits well within 128 subword tokens for most transformer tokenisers, making \texttt{max\_length=128} sufficient for the bulk of the data. However, because the maximum reaches 820 tokens, \texttt{max\_length=256} is the safer default to avoid truncating the longer, elaborately structured PCL paragraphs.

    \item \textbf{Embedding Strategy.} The 26{,}897 unique tokens justify using a \textbf{pre-trained embedding} (e.g.\ GloVe or a BERT sub-word tokeniser) rather than training word embeddings from scratch on this small corpus.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Analysis}

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{N-gram Patterns: Vulnerability-Framing Phrases.} Top bigrams in both classes are dominated by generic English function-word pairs (``of the'', ``in the''), which carry no discriminative power on their own. The signal emerges at the trigram level: phrases such as ``those in need'', ``people in need'', and ``to those in'' construct a narrative of passivity and dependency — a hallmark of patronising language. Non-PCL trigrams, by contrast, are factual and geopolitical: ``the united states'', ``the number of'', ``as well as''. The per-sample rates and enrichment ratios for these trigrams are analysed in detail in point~2 below.

    \item \textbf{Trigram Enrichment: Raw Counts Are Misleading.} ``In need of'' appears 10$\times$ more often in Non-PCL than PCL in absolute terms, yet normalising by class size reveals near-identical per-sample rates (5.3\% PCL vs 5.6\% Non-PCL), making it non-discriminative. By contrast, ``those in need'' is strongly PCL-enriched (PCL 5.0\% vs Non-PCL $\approx$1.2\%, a 4.2$\times$ ratio), as is ``people in need'', which appears in PCL's top~10 but is absent from Non-PCL's. These contrasts are directly visible in the enrichment chart (Figure~\ref{fig:eda2a}, row~3 right). Crucially, all discriminative trigrams consist almost entirely of stop words (``those'', ``in'', ``need'', ``of''), confirming that stop-word removal would destroy the class signal rather than improve the representation.

    \item \textbf{Distinctive Vocabulary (Per-Sample Rates).} After normalising for class size, several words are significantly over-represented in PCL text: ``need'' (2.2$\times$), ``poor'' (2.2$\times$), ``children'', ``homeless'', and ``help'' appear in the PCL top-10 but are absent from the Non-PCL top-10 entirely. This confirms a PCL lexical fingerprint centred on \textit{vulnerability, dependency, and aid}. Non-PCL top words (``immigrants'', ``vulnerable'', ``disabled'') cover the same topic domain but without the emotional framing.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Impact Statement}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Multi-word Patterns are Essential, and Stop Words Must Be Kept.} The discriminative trigrams (``those in need'', ``people in need'', ``to those in'') are composed almost entirely of stop words. A unigram representation would miss these signals entirely; any text representation that strips stop words before computing n-gram features destroys the most discriminative patterns in the corpus. Any model must capture multi-word dependencies to detect these vulnerability-framing phrases.

    \item \textbf{Contextual Embeddings Over Static Representations.} The discriminative PCL vocabulary consists of common words (``need'', ``those'', ``poor'') whose patronising force emerges only in specific multi-word combinations (e.g.\ ``those in need'' vs.\ ``in need of''). Static embeddings assign identical vectors to ``need'' in both contexts, making them unable to capture this distinction. Contextual embeddings from a transformer encoder (RoBERTa) resolve this by dynamically encoding each token in the context of its neighbours, making them particularly well-suited to detecting these pattern-driven PCL signals.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed Approach}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Proposed Approach}

We propose to fine-tune \textbf{RoBERTa-base} \cite{liu2019robertarobustlyoptimizedbert} as the core encoder, augmented with three targeted modifications designed around the structural challenges identified in the EDA: a dual-head multi-task architecture that exploits the dataset's fine-grained label taxonomy, a compound strategy to mitigate the 9.5:1 class imbalance, and context-aware input formatting that injects the vulnerability keyword as an explicit signal.

\subsubsection{Model Architecture}

We plan to attach two lightweight linear heads to the \texttt{[CLS]} token representation of a fine-tuned RoBERTa-base encoder. The \textbf{primary binary head} will produce the PCL / Non-PCL decision used at inference time. In addition, an \textbf{auxiliary multi-label head} will simultaneously predict the 7 fine-grained PCL category labels defined in the \textit{Don't Patronize Me!} dataset \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont} during training. The two heads share the same encoder and are trained jointly under the combined loss:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}$ is Binary Focal Loss \cite{lin2018focallossdenseobject} on the binary head and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}$ is Binary Cross-Entropy on the auxiliary head. The auxiliary head will be discarded at inference; it serves purely as an additional supervision signal during training.

\subsubsection{Data Preprocessing}

Each input will be formatted as \texttt{[keyword] paragraph\_text}, prepending the vulnerability keyword (e.g.\ \textit{homeless}, \textit{immigrants}) as a prefix token. This injects the topic context directly into the sequence without any architectural overhead, aligning the model's input with the annotation process, in which annotators were presented with paragraphs together with their associated keyword. We will use \texttt{max\_length=256} tokens to accommodate the long-tail of longer paragraphs identified in the EDA while keeping memory usage manageable.

\subsubsection{Class-Imbalance Mitigation}

We propose three complementary strategies to address the 9.5:1 class imbalance:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Weighted batch sampling} to oversample PCL positives in every mini-batch, ensuring a consistent minority-class gradient signal throughout training.
    \item \textbf{Focal Loss} \cite{lin2018focallossdenseobject} to down-weight easy-to-classify negatives at the loss level, directing gradient updates toward the harder PCL examples.
    \item \textbf{Post-hoc decision threshold optimisation} by sweeping thresholds on the official dev set after training, replacing the default 0.5 boundary which is biased toward the majority class.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Optimisation Strategy}

We will use AdamW with a \textbf{two-group learning rate schedule}: a lower rate for the pre-trained encoder layers to avoid catastrophic forgetting, and a higher rate for the newly initialised classification heads. A cosine learning rate schedule with linear warmup will be applied, alongside gradient accumulation to achieve a larger effective batch size without requiring additional GPU memory. Early stopping on dev F1 will be used to prevent overfitting.

\subsection{Rationale and Expected Outcome}

\subsubsection{Rationale}

\textbf{Multi-task learning via the auxiliary head.}
The \textit{Don't Patronize Me!} dataset provides 7 fine-grained PCL category labels, including \textit{Unneeded Rescue}, \textit{Shallow Solution}, and \textit{Compassion}, which encode structurally distinct manifestations of patronising language. We hypothesise that training the model to simultaneously predict these categories will force the shared encoder to learn richer, semantically grounded representations of PCL, rather than relying on shallow surface correlations. This is particularly important given the small size of the positive class (794 examples), where additional supervision signal is valuable.

\textbf{Focal Loss and weighted sampling.}
The EDA showed that standard Binary Cross-Entropy on this dataset would concentrate gradient updates overwhelmingly on the 90.5\% Non-PCL majority, pushing the model toward the trivial all-negative prediction. Focal Loss addresses this at the loss level, while the weighted sampler ensures each batch reflects the minority class at the data level. Using both in tandem provides two independent, complementary mechanisms against the imbalance.

\textbf{Keyword prefix injection.}
The lexical EDA demonstrated that discriminative PCL phrases (e.g.\ ``those in need'') co-occur with specific vulnerability topics. Prepending the keyword makes this context explicit and enables the model to learn topic-conditioned PCL patterns. This is a zero-parameter improvement that also aligns the input with how the dataset was annotated.

\textbf{Threshold optimisation.}
The default 0.5 decision threshold is calibrated for balanced classes. For an imbalanced task like PCL detection, sweeping the threshold on the dev set to maximise F1 is expected to yield a meaningful free improvement in the precision--recall trade-off.

\subsubsection{Expected Outcome}

The shared task baseline RoBERTa-base model achieves $\text{F1} = 0.48$ on the official dev set \cite{perez-almendros-etal-2020-dont}. We expect our proposed modifications to deliver a substantial improvement, primarily by increasing the model's recall on PCL-positive examples through better imbalance handling, while the auxiliary multi-task objective and keyword context injection should additionally improve precision by anchoring predictions in structured PCL patterns.

\section{Model Training}
%==============================================================================

The proposed approach was implemented in PyTorch using the HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} library. The full training code and model weights are available in the \texttt{BestModel/} folder of the repository.

\noindent\textbf{Repository Link:} \url{https://github.com/LemonPhase/pcl-detection}

\subsection{Implementation Details}

\subsubsection{Architecture}

RoBERTa-base was loaded from the \texttt{FacebookAI/roberta-base} checkpoint. Two linear heads were attached to the \texttt{[CLS]} token after a shared Dropout layer ($p=0.2$): a binary head \texttt{Linear(768, 1)} and an auxiliary 7-label head \texttt{Linear(768, 7)}. Both heads were initialised with small normal weights (std $= 0.02$) and zero biases for training stability.

\subsubsection{Data Split and Evaluation Protocol}

The official training set (8,375 samples) was used for training and the official dev set (2094 samples) was used as the held-out evaluation set throughout all experiments; no internal dev split was created, as the official dev set provides a stable benchmark comparable across runs. The official test set (3,832 samples, labels withheld) was used only for final prediction.

After training, a threshold sweep over $t \in [0.05, 0.95]$ (step 0.01) was run on the dev set to select the decision boundary maximising PCL-class F1.

\subsubsection{Insights and Discussion}

The local evaluation reveals two distinct failure modes:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Lexical over-triggering (FPs):} The model has learned strong associations between vulnerability-topic vocabulary (``those in need'', ``poor families'') and the PCL label, causing it to fire on factual or spiritually framed text sharing the same surface forms. This is a direct consequence of keyword prefix injection: the {\texttt{[in-need]}} prefix activates PCL-associated representations even when the text is non-patronising. A potential mitigation would be to include more lexically diverse Non-PCL examples during training.
    \item \textbf{Implicit PCL blindness (FNs):} The model largely fails to detect PCL encoded in narrative framing, assumed premises, or the writer's self-positioning (\textit{The Poorer, the Merrier}, \textit{Presupposition}, \textit{Authority Voice}). These categories do not rely on a consistent surface vocabulary, making them difficult to detect from the {\texttt{[CLS]}} token alone. Addressing this would likely require richer contextual modelling, for example attention over the full paragraph to capture narrative arc, or targeted data augmentation.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the model is conservative and specific: it reliably detects \textbf{explicit, vocabulary-driven PCL} (particularly in \texttt{in-need} contexts) but struggles with the more linguistically sophisticated forms of patronising language that dominate the tail of the error distribution.

\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

This report addressed binary Patronising and Condescending Language (PCL) detection on the \textit{Don't Patronize Me!}\ dataset. The EDA established two key structural challenges---a severe 9.5:1 class imbalance and a discriminative signal carried by multi-word, stop-word-heavy phrases rather than individual vocabulary items---that directly motivated the design choices in our proposed model.

The submitted system fine-tunes RoBERTa-base with a multi-task dual-head architecture, Focal Loss, weighted batch sampling, keyword prefix injection, and post-hoc threshold optimisation. The best checkpoint, saved at epoch 7 of 10, achieves $\text{F1} = 0.606$ on the official dev set, a $\sim$26\% relative improvement over the 0.48 baseline. The model is conservative and high-precision (precision 0.649, recall 0.568), operating with a very low false positive rate of 3.2\%.

Error analysis reveals two distinct failure modes that point to the path forward. First, lexical over-triggering causes false positives on factual text that shares PCL vocabulary but not PCL intent; this is partly a side-effect of the keyword prefix and could be mitigated with more lexically diverse non-PCL training examples. Second, the model is largely blind to structurally implicit PCL (\textit{Presupposition}, \textit{The Poorer, the Merrier}) where condescension is encoded in narrative framing rather than surface vocabulary; addressing these categories would require richer discourse-level modelling or targeted data augmentation.

Future work could explore span-level supervision (exploiting the available annotation spans), a lower decision threshold to trade precision for recall in recall-critical applications, and RoBERTa-large for additional encoder capacity on the implicit PCL categories.
